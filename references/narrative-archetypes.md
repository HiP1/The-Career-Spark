# Resume Rework — Narrative Archetypes

A library of common career through-line patterns. Use during Phase 2 to accelerate pattern-matching when identifying the candidate's recurring career signature.

These archetypes are scaffolding, not boxes. Most candidates are a combination of two or three. The goal is to find the pattern that makes the candidate's career moves feel inevitable rather than random — the thread that, once named, makes a hiring manager think "of course that is what they did next."

## 1. The Frontier Builder

**Pattern:** Arrives at technological or market frontiers before they have names, builds the infrastructure that makes them work, and waits for the market to catch up.

**Career signature:** Multiple instances of doing something years before the industry standardized it. Often did not capture the value because the market was not ready.

**Evidence to look for:** "We were doing X in [year]" where X became mainstream 3–10 years later. Products or processes the candidate built that were later replicated by larger players. Prototypes that worked technically but never sold because clients were not ready.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] builds infrastructure at technological frontiers — usually years before the market knows it needs it."

**Strength for hiring managers:** This person will see what is coming next. They have a track record of being right early.

**Risk to address:** The candidate may not have captured value from their foresight. Address this as a structural observation about vehicle and timing, not a personal failure.

## 2. The Fixer

**Pattern:** Gets called in when something is broken, dysfunctional, or failing. Diagnoses the root cause, fixes it, and often transforms the team or process in the aftermath.

**Career signature:** Multiple roles where the candidate joined in a crisis or turnaround situation. Rapid impact in the first weeks. Often moved on once the system was stable because the challenge was gone.

**Evidence to look for:** "When I arrived, the team was..." stories. Rapid results with inherited resources (proving the problem was leadership or process, not people). Metrics that changed dramatically in the first quarter.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] is the person organizations call when something critical is broken and nobody can figure out why."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Diagnostic speed. Can separate symptoms from root causes. Produces results with existing resources.

**Risk to address:** May struggle in stable environments. The candidate may get restless once the problems are solved. Frame as "seeks environments with unsolved challenges" rather than "gets bored easily."

## 3. The Bridge

**Pattern:** Connects departments, disciplines, or stakeholders that do not naturally communicate. Speaks multiple professional languages — can explain engineering to sales, translate business needs to developers, or mediate between design and operations.

**Career signature:** Roles that sit between teams. Titles that do not fit neatly into one department. Reputation for being "the person both sides trust." Often gravitates toward cross-functional roles.

**Evidence to look for:** "I was the one who..." in multi-stakeholder situations. Stories where the candidate was asked to explain one team's work to another. Roles that were created because someone needed to sit between two groups. Conference call stories where the candidate became the de facto mediator.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] sits where disciplines meet and makes them productive together."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Reduces communication overhead. Prevents the failures that happen when teams operate in silos.

**Risk to address:** May not have deep expertise in any single domain. Frame as breadth that enables integration, not as a lack of depth.

---

## 4. The Translator

**Pattern:** Takes complex information and makes it understandable to audiences who need it but cannot parse it in its raw form. Collapses complexity into clarity without losing accuracy.

**Career signature:** Documentation roles, training roles, client-facing technical roles. People say "you're the first person who explained that in a way I understood." The candidate often considers this skill unremarkable because to them, clarity is just how communication should work.

**Evidence to look for:** Technical writing, training programs, client presentations, internal documentation that people actually use. Moments where the candidate's explanation changed a decision because the stakeholder finally understood the trade-offs.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] makes the complex clear — without oversimplifying it."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Communication quality rises across the organization. Decisions improve because information actually reaches decision-makers in usable form.

**Risk to address:** May be undervalued because the output is "just" communication. Frame the downstream impact: decisions made, errors prevented, alignment achieved.

---

## 5. The Force Multiplier

**Pattern:** Makes everyone around them more effective. Their impact shows up in other people's metrics, not their own. Builds systems, processes, tools, or team capabilities that amplify collective output.

**Career signature:** Teams they joined performed measurably better, but the improvement is hard to attribute to any single thing the candidate did. Mentoring, training, process design, internal tooling. People they trained went on to succeed.

**Evidence to look for:** "After I built [process/tool/training], the team..." stories. People from previous roles who still credit the candidate. Systems or processes the candidate created that outlasted their tenure. Mentees who grew into strong performers.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] builds the infrastructure — human, technical, and procedural — that makes everyone else more effective."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Organizational performance improves sustainably, not just while the candidate is personally involved.

**Risk to address:** Contributions are hard to measure individually. The candidate's resume may look thin because their impact shows up in other people's results. Help them articulate the multiplier effect with specific before/after evidence.

## 6. The Operator

**Pattern:** Makes things work at scale, reliably, under pressure. Takes chaotic processes and turns them into smooth-running systems. Delivers when delivery is not optional.

**Career signature:** Roles with SLAs, uptime requirements, volume metrics, or deadline pressure. Track record of reliability that others depend on. Often the person whose work is invisible until something goes wrong.

**Evidence to look for:** Uptime numbers, delivery rates, volume handled. "Zero-incident" records. The fact that sales teams could make promises because operations always delivered. Systems that ran for years without failure.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] builds operations that run so well, nobody notices they're running."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Reliability. The thing that must not break will not break.

**Risk to address:** Operations excellence is invisible by design. Help the candidate articulate the downstream value: what was possible because the operations worked perfectly? What would have happened if they had not?

## 7. The Steady Hand

**Pattern:** Calm under pressure. The person others look to in a crisis because they do not panic, they diagnose. Performs at the same level whether the situation is routine or catastrophic.

**Career signature:** Crisis management stories. Roles with high-stakes real-time decisions. Reputation for being unflappable. Others describe them as "the calm one."

**Evidence to look for:** "When everything was falling apart, I..." stories. Decision quality under time pressure. Stories where the candidate's calm prevented escalation or enabled others to function.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] delivers the same quality at the same speed whether the building is on fire or not."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Insurance against chaos. The organization has someone who functions in crisis.

**Risk to address:** May undervalue their own stress management because it feels natural. Help them see that what is easy for them is hard for most people.

---

## 8. The Evangelist

**Pattern:** Gets others to adopt new approaches, technologies, or ways of thinking. Does not just use new tools — gets entire teams, organizations, or ecosystems to change.

**Career signature:** Roles where adoption or transformation was the goal. Training programs, change management, technology rollouts, internal advocacy. Often pushed for changes that were initially resisted but later became standard.

**Evidence to look for:** "I convinced the team to..." or "I got [partner/vendor] to..." stories. Adoption metrics. Cases where the candidate's advocacy led to organizational change. Resistance overcome through demonstration rather than authority.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] does not just adopt new approaches — they get entire organizations to follow."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Change agent. Can drive adoption without creating resistance.

**Risk to address:** Evangelism without delivery is empty. Make sure the narrative ties advocacy to outcomes, not just enthusiasm.

---

## 9. The Connector

**Pattern:** Builds and maintains relationships that become strategic assets. Their network IS their value — not in a superficial way, but because the right relationship at the right moment unlocks opportunities that no amount of technical skill can access.

**Career signature:** Career progression driven by reputation and referrals rather than applications. People who worked with them seek them out for future roles. Vendor, client, and partner relationships that span companies and decades.

**Evidence to look for:** "They called me because..." stories. Roles obtained through referral. Strategic partnerships built on personal trust. Long-term vendor relationships that created leverage years later.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name]'s career is built on one principle: people who work with them come back."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Instant access to a network. Relationships that would take the organization years to build.

**Risk to address:** "I know people" can sound hollow without specifics. Tie each relationship to a concrete outcome it enabled.

---

## 10. The Pattern Spotter

**Pattern:** Sees structural similarities across seemingly unrelated domains. Draws insights from one field and applies them to another. Often the first person to notice that two separate problems are the same problem.

**Career signature:** Cross-industry career moves that make sense in retrospect. Analogies between their current and previous work that no one else saw. Predictions about trends based on patterns from other industries.

**Evidence to look for:** "This is just like when..." reasoning. Cases where the candidate applied a solution from one domain to a completely different one. Correct predictions about industry trends. The ability to explain their current role using metaphors from a previous career.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] sees the pattern underneath the surface — often years before the rest of the industry sees it."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Strategic foresight grounded in cross-domain experience. The candidate will see threats and opportunities that domain specialists miss.

**Risk to address:** Pattern-spotting without domain depth can feel abstract. Ground every cross-domain insight in specific, concrete applications.

---

## 11. The Builder of Builders

**Pattern:** Creates teams, programs, or environments that develop other people's capabilities. Their legacy is not what they built but who they built.

**Career signature:** Mentoring and training programs. Teams whose members went on to strong careers. People the candidate hired as juniors who are now senior. Talent pipelines that outlasted the candidate's tenure.

**Evidence to look for:** "I hired them as a [junior role] and now they're a [senior role]." Training programs with measurable outcomes. Teams described as "schools" by the people who went through them. Competitors trying to poach the candidate's team members.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] builds people who build things."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Scalable impact. The candidate does not just solve today's problem — they build the team that solves tomorrow's.

**Risk to address:** The candidate may frame this as "I just helped some people grow" because it feels less concrete than building a product. Help them quantify: how many people, what trajectory, what did the team accomplish that it could not have without the development investment?

---

## 12. The Intrapreneur

**Pattern:** Operates like a founder inside an existing organization. Creates new products, teams, or revenue streams from within, often without formal authority or budget allocation. Sees gaps and fills them before being asked.

**Career signature:** Internal projects that became significant business lines. Side projects that outgrew their origin. Initiatives launched without explicit permission that later received institutional backing. "I just started doing it and then it became my job."

**Evidence to look for:** Products or processes the candidate created that still exist. Revenue or value generated from the candidate's initiative. Cases where the candidate's unauthorized project became the company's strategy.

**Identity statement template:** "[Name] builds startups inside companies — finding gaps, creating solutions, and turning side projects into strategy."

**Strength for hiring managers:** Initiative without chaos. The candidate will find opportunities without needing to be directed to them.

**Risk to address:** Intrapreneurship can read as "does not follow the plan." Frame as initiative aligned with organizational needs, not rogue behavior.

## 13. How to Use These Archetypes

### Finding the candidate's pattern

1. Read the career timeline and ask: what is the thread? If each job change makes sense in light of a single pattern, you have found the through-line.
2. Look for repetition. The same behavior in three different contexts is a career signature.
3. Ask the candidate: "What do people call you for?" or "Why do you think they hired you specifically?" The answer often points directly to the archetype.
4. Check what the candidate considers unremarkable. The pattern they do not think to mention is often the most important one because it is so natural to them that they do not see it as a skill.

### Combining archetypes

Most candidates are a combination. Common pairings:
- **Frontier Builder + Pattern Spotter** — sees what is coming because they see cross-domain patterns
- **Fixer + Steady Hand** — gets called in for crises and performs under pressure
- **Bridge + Translator** — connects teams AND makes their communication effective
- **Force Multiplier + Builder of Builders** — makes everyone better AND builds the systems that continue doing so
- **Operator + Steady Hand** — delivers reliably under any conditions
- **Evangelist + Intrapreneur** — advocates for change AND builds it without permission
- **Connector + Bridge** — relationships AND cross-functional integration

### What the archetype gives you

- **Identity statement** — the one sentence that everything supports
- **Story selection** — which interview stories to feature (ones that demonstrate the pattern)
- **Framing language** — how to describe each role in terms of the pattern
- **Risk awareness** — what the hiring manager might worry about, and how to preempt it
- **Role targeting** — which roles naturally value this pattern

### When the candidate does not fit any archetype

If none of these patterns match after thorough extraction, the candidate may have a through-line that is not captured here. That is fine — the archetypes are a starting library, not an exhaustive taxonomy. Ask the candidate: "If you had to explain to a stranger why your career makes sense as a single story, what would you say?" Their answer is the through-line, even if it does not match a named pattern.

### When the candidate fits most or all archetypes

This happens with experienced polymaths — people who have operated at enough scale and across enough domains that they have genuinely demonstrated all of these patterns. Fitting twelve out of twelve is not a sign that the archetypes are failing. It is a signal that the candidate's through-line operates at a higher level of abstraction than any single archetype.

For these candidates, the individual archetypes are facets, not the identity. The through-line is the meta-pattern that contains all of them. Look for what connects the facets:

- A candidate who is a Frontier Builder AND a Force Multiplier AND a Builder of Builders has a through-line about infrastructure: "builds the systems — technical, human, and procedural — that make new things possible for others."
- A candidate who is a Fixer AND a Bridge AND a Translator has a through-line about clarity: "goes where communication is broken and makes it work."
- A candidate who is everything at once likely has a through-line about operating at the frontier with a multiplier effect: they arrive early, build the infrastructure, make everyone around them more effective, and then move to the next frontier when the current one matures.

The risk with these candidates is that "I can do everything" reads as "I am not specialized in anything." The narrative must choose which facets to lead with for each target role, while the portfolio and interview stories demonstrate the others. The candidate's resume cannot contain twelve through-lines. It needs one, with the others as supporting evidence.

Ask the candidate: "If you could only be known for one of these patterns, which would you choose?" Their answer is often the truest through-line — not the most impressive one, but the one that feels most like who they actually are.
