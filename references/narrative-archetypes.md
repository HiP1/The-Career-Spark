# The Career Spark — Narrative Archetypes

Common career through-line patterns. Use in Phase 2 to accelerate pattern-matching. Archetypes are scaffolding, not boxes. Most candidates are a combination of 2–3.

---

## 1. The Frontier Builder

Arrives at frontiers before they have names, builds the infrastructure, waits for the market to catch up.

**Evidence:** "We were doing X in [year]" where X became mainstream years later. Prototypes that worked but never sold because the market was not ready.
**Identity template:** "[Name] builds infrastructure at technological frontiers. Usually years before the market needs it."
**Risk to address:** May not have captured value. Frame as structural (vehicle and timing), not personal failure.

## 2. The Fixer

Gets called when something is broken. Diagnoses root cause, fixes it, transforms the aftermath.

**Evidence:** Crisis/turnaround entry points. Rapid first-quarter impact. Metrics changing dramatically with inherited resources.
**Identity template:** "[Name] is the person organizations call when something critical is broken."
**Risk:** May struggle in stable environments. Frame as "seeks unsolved challenges."

## 3. The Bridge

Connects departments or stakeholders that do not naturally communicate. Speaks multiple professional languages.

**Evidence:** Roles between teams. "The person both sides trust." Multi-stakeholder mediation. Roles created to sit between groups.
**Identity template:** "[Name] sits where disciplines meet and makes them productive together."
**Risk:** May lack deep single-domain expertise. Frame as breadth enabling integration.

## 4. The Translator

Takes complexity and makes it understandable without losing accuracy.

**Evidence:** "You're the first person who explained that." Documentation people actually use. Explanations that changed decisions.
**Identity template:** "[Name] makes the complex clear without oversimplifying."
**Risk:** May be undervalued ("just" communication). Frame the downstream impact.

## 5. The Force Multiplier

Makes everyone around them more effective. Impact shows in other people's metrics.

**Evidence:** Teams performed better after they joined. Mentees who succeeded. Systems/processes that outlasted their tenure.
**Identity template:** "[Name] builds infrastructure (human, technical, procedural) that makes everyone else more effective."
**Risk:** Hard to measure individually. Help articulate the multiplier effect.

## 6. The Operator

Makes things work at scale, reliably, under pressure. Turns chaos into smooth systems.

**Evidence:** Uptime, delivery rates, volume. "Zero-incident" records. Sales promises that could be made because operations always delivered.
**Identity template:** "[Name] builds operations that run so well, nobody notices."
**Risk:** Operations excellence is invisible by design. Articulate what it enabled.

## 7. The Steady Hand

Calm under pressure. Same performance level in routine and crisis.

**Evidence:** Crisis management. Decision quality under time pressure. Others describing them as "the calm one."
**Identity template:** "[Name] delivers the same quality whether the building is on fire or not."
**Risk:** May undervalue stress management because it feels natural.

## 8. The Evangelist

Gets others to adopt new approaches. Not just uses new tools. Drives organizational change.

**Evidence:** "I convinced the team to..." Adoption metrics. Resistance overcome through demonstration not authority.
**Identity template:** "[Name] does not just adopt new approaches. They get organizations to follow."
**Risk:** Tie advocacy to outcomes, not just enthusiasm.

## 9. The Connector

Builds relationships that become strategic assets. Their network IS their value.

**Evidence:** Career progression through referral. People seeking them out for future roles. Vendor/partner relationships spanning decades.
**Identity template:** "[Name]'s career is built on one principle: people who work with them come back."
**Risk:** "I know people" needs specific outcomes to be credible.

## 10. The Pattern Spotter

Sees structural similarities across unrelated domains. Applies solutions from one field to another.

**Evidence:** Cross-industry career moves that make sense in retrospect. Correct predictions from pattern transfer. "This is just like when..."
**Identity template:** "[Name] sees the pattern underneath. Often years before the industry sees it."
**Risk:** Pattern-spotting without domain depth feels abstract. Ground every insight in concrete applications.

## 11. The Builder of Builders

Creates teams and environments that develop people. Legacy is who they built, not what.

**Evidence:** Mentees who advanced. Teams described as "schools." Competitors trying to poach team members. "I hired them as [junior], now they're [senior]."
**Identity template:** "[Name] builds people who build things."
**Risk:** Feels less concrete than building a product. Quantify: how many people, what trajectory.

## 12. The Intrapreneur

Operates like a founder inside an existing organization. Creates products, teams, or revenue streams from within.

**Evidence:** Internal projects that became business lines. "I just started doing it and then it became my job." Revenue from the candidate's initiative.
**Identity template:** "[Name] builds startups inside companies."
**Risk:** Can read as "doesn't follow the plan." Frame as initiative aligned with organizational needs.

---

## Using These Archetypes

**Finding the pattern:** What is the thread across jobs? What repeats in three different contexts? Ask: "What do people call you for?" Check what the candidate considers unremarkable. Often the most important pattern.

**Combining:** Common pairings: Frontier Builder + Pattern Spotter, Fixer + Steady Hand, Bridge + Translator, Force Multiplier + Builder of Builders, Evangelist + Intrapreneur.

**When the candidate fits most or all:** This happens with experienced polymaths. The through-line is above the archetype level. Individual archetypes are facets; the identity statement is the meta-pattern containing them. Ask: "If you could only be known for one of these, which would you choose?" Their answer is often the truest through-line.

**When none fit:** Ask: "If you had to explain to a stranger why your career makes sense as a single story, what would you say?" Their answer is the through-line.
