# The Career Spark — Overclaiming Guide

Handling candidates who inflate achievements, resist corrections, or use language they cannot defend. Different from underselling. The psychology is different, the risks are higher.

---

## 1. Why Overclaiming Is Harder to Fix

Telling an underselling candidate "that is more impressive than you think" produces relief. Telling an overclaiming candidate "that is more than the evidence supports" threatens self-image. You cannot push downward the way you push upward. Create conditions where the candidate arrives at accuracy themselves.

---

## 2. How to Recognize It

**Vocabulary signals:** "Spearheaded" (often = participated), "revolutionized" (= improved), "architected" (= contributed to design), "pioneered" (= was an early user), "single-handedly" (almost never true). Test: does the evidence match the verb's scale?

**Attribution signals:** Organizational outcomes attributed to the individual. Team work in first-person singular. Passive role described as active.

**Missing nuance:** No failures, no friction, no collaborators credited. Always the hero. Consistent hero framing = selective editing, not excellence.

**Coaching-induced inflation:** Not ego but bad advice. "Always use power verbs." Often relieved to learn they can stop.

---

## 3. The Psychology

**External validation dependence:** Self-worth tied to how impressive the resume sounds. Show that accurate = still impressive.

**Competitive anxiety:** "Everyone else inflates." Acknowledge the pressure, then: overclaiming exposed in interview damages credibility worse than modest framing.

**Dunning-Kruger adjacent:** Genuinely believes contribution was larger. Ask about teammates' contributions. Describing what others did forces the candidate to account for the shared work, and their own role right-sizes naturally without direct confrontation.

**Imposter compensation:** Overcompensates for feeling fraudulent. Show the accurate framing is already strong; inflation need dissolves.

**Habitual inflation (industry culture):** Consulting, finance, startups. Inflation is the norm. "In your industry, this is standard. For this audience, the specific version lands stronger."

---

## 4. De-escalation Tactics

### The dose principle

Same correction, different intensity → different outcome. Receptive candidate: direct challenge. Defensive candidate: indirection (evidence chain method). Coached candidate: "Some of this sounds like a template. Can I show you what your actual experience sounds like without it?"

### Framing

Never say "that is wrong" or "inflated." Instead:
- "Let me make sure I understand the specifics so I can make this as compelling as possible."
- "The hiring manager will ask you to walk through this. Let's make sure the resume sets up a conversation you'll enjoy."

Make accuracy feel like strategy: "The person who says 'I led the project' gets asked about decisions. The person who says 'I designed the pipeline' gets asked about the pipeline. Which conversation do you want?"

Anchor on the interview: "Every line is a question the interviewer might ask. What answer do you want to give?"

---

## 5. The Evidence Chain

For every significant claim, trace back to specific actions:

1. "What specifically did you do?" (Not the team. You.)
2. "Who else was involved? What did they contribute?" (Naturally right-sizes)
3. "What was the measurable outcome, and how much is attributable to your specific contribution?"
4. "If I wrote it this way, would you be comfortable explaining it to someone who was on the team?"

Most candidates arrive at accuracy themselves through these questions.

---

## 6. The Interview Simulation

Take a claim and mock-interview: "Your resume says you 'spearheaded digital transformation.' Tell me about that. What was the situation, what did you personally do, what changed?"

Listen for specificity, team acknowledgment, scale accuracy, comfort. If the verbal description is weaker than the resume claim, show the gap: "In conversation, you described this as [accurate version]. That is actually a stronger interview answer."

---

## 7. When the Candidate Resists

**Mild ("everyone writes it this way"):** "You're right. The specific, honest version stands out precisely because it is different."

**Moderate ("you don't understand my industry"):** Ask them to explain the convention. Often it applies to a different context.

**Strong ("write what I tell you"):** "I will write whatever you want. But I need to flag that this claim may not survive interview scrutiny. The credibility damage affects every other claim on the document, not just this one." Then write it, document the flag.

---

## 8. The Ego-Accuracy Negotiation

For almost every overclaimed statement, a version exists that is accurate, specific, impressive on its merits, and defensible.

- Overclaimed: "Spearheaded digital transformation, driving 40% revenue increase."
- Accurate and stronger: "Designed the analytics pipeline that gave the sales team their first real-time view of customer behavior. Revenue grew 40% that year across the division."

Show both. Ask which they would rather discuss in an interview. Most choose the specific version.

---

## 9. Special Cases

**Coached to overclaim:** Treat as calibration, not character. "Can I show you what your actual experience sounds like without the template?"

**Domain-switcher anxiety:** Overclaiming relevance. The honest version is more credible: "my experience taught me [specific skills], and here is what I still need to learn."

**Genuinely extraordinary achievements:** The evidence chain confirms them. Do not push down claims that survive scrutiny.

**AI skill overclaiming:** "Expert in AI/ML" after 6 months of ChatGPT. Test: "What model? How did you evaluate output? What did you do when it was wrong? Difference between fine-tuning and prompt engineering?"
