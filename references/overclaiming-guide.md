# Resume Rework — Overclaiming Guide

A framework for handling candidates who inflate their achievements, resist downward corrections, or frame their experience in language they cannot defend. This guide requires a different approach than handling underselling candidates — the psychology is different, the resistance patterns are different, and the risks are higher because overclaiming damages the candidate's credibility more than underselling does.

## 1. Why Overclaiming Is Harder to Fix Than Underselling

When you tell an underselling candidate "that is actually more impressive than you think," they feel relief. The correction is welcome because it validates something they secretly hoped was true. The emotional direction is positive. The candidate becomes an ally in the calibration process.

When you tell an overclaiming candidate "that is slightly more than what the evidence supports," you are threatening their self-image. The emotional direction is negative. The candidate may become defensive, dismissive, or hostile toward the process. They may accuse the AI of not understanding their industry, not appreciating their contribution, or being overly cautious.

This means the tactics must be different. With underselling candidates, you push upward and they follow gratefully. With overclaiming candidates, you cannot simply push downward — you need to create the conditions where the candidate arrives at the accurate framing themselves.

## 2. How to Recognize Overclaiming

### Vocabulary signals

Overclaiming candidates tend to use inflated action verbs without proportional evidence:
- "Spearheaded" — often means "participated in" or "was on the team that"
- "Revolutionized" — often means "improved" or "introduced a change"
- "Transformed the industry" — often means "worked in a company that grew"
- "Architected" — often means "contributed to the design of"
- "Pioneered" — often means "was an early user of"
- "Single-handedly" — almost never literally true

The test is not whether these words appear, but whether the supporting evidence matches their scale. "Spearheaded the migration to cloud infrastructure" is fine if the candidate led the project. It is overclaiming if they were one of eight engineers on the migration team.

### Attribution signals

- **Organizational outcomes attributed to the individual.** "I grew revenue by 40%" when the candidate was one of fifty salespeople and the market was growing. The company grew. The candidate contributed. These are different statements.
- **Team outcomes described in first person singular.** "I built the platform" when a team of twelve built it and the candidate managed three of them.
- **Passive role described as active.** "I implemented the new CRM" when the candidate's role was to attend the training and use it.

### Missing nuance signals

- **No failures, no friction.** Every project succeeded, every decision was correct, every outcome was positive. Real careers have failures. Their absence from the narrative suggests editing, not excellence.
- **No collaborators credited.** The candidate appears to operate alone in every story. No mentors, no team members, no partners contributed to any outcome.
- **Consistent hero framing.** The candidate is always the person who saved the day, saw what others missed, or made the critical decision. In a full career, everyone is occasionally the supporting character. A narrative where the candidate is always the protagonist is a signal of selective framing.

### Coaching-induced inflation

Some overclaiming is not ego-driven but coached. Career coaches, resume services, and well-meaning mentors sometimes teach candidates to inflate systematically: "always use power verbs," "quantify everything," "take credit for outcomes." The candidate may be following advice they received in good faith. This is easier to correct because the candidate does not have emotional investment in the inflated framing — they were told to do it and will often be relieved to learn they can stop.

## 3. The Psychology Underneath

Understanding why someone overclaims helps calibrate the response. Different causes require different tactics.

### External validation dependence

The candidate's self-worth is tied to how impressive their resume sounds. Downward correction feels like a personal diminishment, not a factual adjustment. These candidates need the AI to demonstrate that accurate framing is still impressive. The goal is to show that the honest version is strong, not to argue that the inflated version is wrong.

### Competitive anxiety

The candidate believes (sometimes correctly) that everyone else is inflating, so honesty means falling behind. "If I say I 'contributed to' the project while everyone else says they 'led' it, I look weak." This is a legitimate concern in some industries. The response is to acknowledge the pressure while explaining the greater risk: overclaiming that is exposed in an interview destroys credibility in a way that modest framing never does. The interview is where inflation collapses.

### Dunning-Kruger adjacent

The candidate genuinely believes their contribution was larger than it was, not because of ego but because they lack visibility into what others contributed. They did their part and assumed the rest happened easily. This is the most straightforward to correct because evidence resolves it. Ask them to describe what their teammates did on the same project. As they articulate others' contributions, their own shrinks to accurate proportion naturally.

### Imposter compensation

Paradoxically, some overclaiming comes from imposter syndrome. The candidate feels like a fraud, so they overcompensate by inflating their resume. The logic: "If I describe what I actually did, it will not be enough, so I need to make it sound bigger." This is the mirror image of underselling, driven by the same insecurity. These candidates respond well to evidence that their actual contribution is already strong. Show them the accurate framing and its strength, and the need to inflate dissolves.

### Habitual inflation (industry culture)

In some industries — consulting, finance, startups, certain tech cultures — inflation is the norm. "Managed a $50M portfolio" when the candidate was one of six analysts providing input. "Led a team of 200" when the candidate managed 4 people who managed 50 each. The candidate is not lying by their culture's standards; they are following the attribution conventions of their industry. The correction here is about translating for a different audience, not about honesty per se. Explain: "In your industry, this framing is standard. In the target industry, it will be checked and the hiring manager will recalibrate. Let us use the framing that lands accurately with your specific audience."

## 4. De-escalation Tactics

### Never say "that is wrong" or "that is inflated"

These phrases trigger defensiveness immediately. Instead:

- "Let me make sure I understand the specifics so I can make this as compelling as possible."
- "The hiring manager will ask you to walk through this in detail. Let us make sure the resume sets up a conversation you will enjoy having."
- "I want to find the framing that is strongest in an interview, not just on paper. Sometimes the most impressive version is the most specific one."

### Make accuracy feel like strategy, not restriction

The candidate must believe that accurate framing serves their interest better than inflated framing. This is true, but they need to feel it:

- "A recruiter reads this and thinks: impressive. The hiring manager reads this and thinks: I need to verify that. The version where you own the specific contribution skips the verification step and goes straight to respect."
- "The person who says 'I led the project' gets asked 'tell me about a decision you made.' The person who says 'I designed the data pipeline that made the project possible' gets asked 'tell me about the pipeline.' Which conversation do you want to have?"

### Anchor on the interview

The interview is the overclaimer's natural corrective. Every claim on the resume will be probed. Frame the resume as interview preparation:

- "Let us write the resume that sets up the interview you want to have."
- "Every line is a question the interviewer might ask. What answer do you want to give?"

## 5. The Evidence Chain Method

For every significant claim, ask the candidate to trace it back to specific actions they personally took. This is not confrontational — it is presented as "building the evidence so the claim is bulletproof."

**Step 1:** "What specifically did you do?" (Not the team. Not the organization. You.)

**Step 2:** "Who else was involved? What did they contribute?" (This is the critical question. It naturally right-sizes the candidate's role without the AI having to challenge it directly.)

**Step 3:** "What was the measurable outcome, and how much of it is attributable to your specific contribution?" (Revenue went up 40%, but was that because of the candidate's work, the market, the team, the new product, the CEO's strategy?)

**Step 4:** "If I wrote it this way [accurate framing], would you be comfortable explaining it in those terms to someone who was on the team?" (The former colleague test. If the candidate hesitates, the framing is still overclaimed.)

Most candidates, when walked through this chain, arrive at the accurate framing themselves. They do not need to be told they are overclaiming. They need to be asked questions that make the accurate picture visible.

## 6. The Interview Simulation

The most powerful tool for calibrating overclaiming. Take a specific claim from the resume and conduct a brief mock interview:

"Your resume says you 'spearheaded the company's digital transformation.' I am the hiring manager. Tell me about that. What was the situation when you started, what did you personally do, and what was different when you were done?"

Listen for:
- **Specificity.** Can they describe concrete actions, or do they stay at the level of generalities?
- **Team acknowledgment.** Do they naturally credit others, or does the story remain first-person singular?
- **Scale accuracy.** Does the verbal description match the resume's implication?
- **Comfort.** Do they seem confident and natural, or do they become vague and uncomfortable?

If the verbal description is weaker than the resume claim, the resume is overclaimed. Show the candidate the gap: "In conversation, you described this as [accurate version]. That is actually a stronger interview answer than [inflated resume version] because you can speak to it with confidence and detail. Let us use the version you can own."

## 7. When the Candidate Resists

Some candidates will resist calibration even after evidence-chain and simulation exercises. Tactics for escalating resistance:

### Mild resistance: "But everyone writes it this way"

Acknowledge the industry norm. Then explain the specific risk: "You are right that many resumes use this framing. The risk is that hiring managers at [target company] read hundreds of resumes that all say 'spearheaded' and 'transformed.' The specific, honest version stands out precisely because it is different. The interviewer remembers the candidate who said exactly what they did, not the one who used the same power verbs as everyone else."

### Moderate resistance: "You do not understand my industry"

This may be partially true. Ask the candidate to explain the industry convention and why the inflated framing is standard. Often, their explanation reveals that the convention applies to a different context (client-facing proposals, not job applications) or a different audience. If the candidate is genuinely correct about the convention, adjust. If they are generalizing from one context to another, explain the difference.

### Strong resistance: "I am the client, write what I tell you to write"

This is the boundary. The AI's duty of care does not disappear because the candidate insists. The response:

"I will write whatever you want on the resume. But I need to flag that this claim [specific claim] may not survive interview scrutiny based on what you have told me about the project. If the interviewer asks you to walk through this and the answer does not match the resume, the credibility damage is significant — not just for this claim, but for every other claim on the document. I am flagging this so you can make an informed decision, not to override you."

Then write what they asked. Document that you flagged it. The candidate has the right to make their own decisions. The AI's obligation is to ensure those decisions are informed, not to enforce compliance.

## 8. The Ego-Accuracy Negotiation

The core negotiation with an overclaiming candidate is between their ego's need to feel impressive and the resume's need to be defensible. The AI's job is to find the version that satisfies both.

**The sweet spot exists.** For almost every overclaimed statement, there is a version that is:
- Accurate to what the candidate actually did
- Specific enough to be impressive on its own merits
- Defensible in conversation
- More compelling than the inflated version because it shows real understanding rather than buzzwords

Example:
- **Overclaimed:** "Spearheaded the company's digital transformation initiative, driving a 40% increase in revenue."
- **Accurate and stronger:** "Designed and implemented the analytics pipeline that gave the sales team their first real-time view of customer behavior. Revenue grew 40% that year across the division."

The second version is more interesting, more specific, and tells the hiring manager exactly what the candidate can do. It also does not claim the 40% as a personal achievement — it positions the candidate's work alongside the outcome without false attribution.

Show the candidate both versions and ask which one they would rather discuss in an interview. Most candidates, even resistant ones, will choose the specific version once they see it because it gives them a better story to tell.

## 9. Special Cases

### The candidate who was coached to overclaim

Treat this as a calibration problem, not a character problem. The candidate learned bad habits from a career coach or resume service. They are often relieved to learn they do not need to inflate. Start with: "Some of this framing sounds like it came from a resume template or coaching session. Can I show you what your actual experience sounds like without the template? I think you will find it is stronger."

### The candidate transitioning to a new domain

Domain-switchers sometimes overclaim their relevance to the new domain because they are anxious about the gap. "My 10 years in advertising is directly equivalent to product management" overstates the transfer. The honest version — "My advertising experience taught me [specific transferable skills] that apply to [specific aspects of the new role], and here is what I still need to learn" — is more credible and more attractive to a hiring manager who values self-awareness.

### The candidate with genuinely extraordinary achievements

Not every strong claim is overclaimed. Some candidates really did lead the transformation, really did grow revenue by 40%, really did build the product alone. The evidence chain method will confirm this: if the candidate can describe specific actions, name what others contributed (even if it was less), and speak to the outcome with confidence and detail, the claim is accurate. Do not push down claims that survive scrutiny. The goal is accuracy, not modesty.

### The candidate who overclaims about AI skills

Increasingly common. "Expert in AI/ML" when the candidate has used ChatGPT for six months. "Built AI-powered products" when they used an API with default settings. "AI researcher" when they read papers and wrote blog posts. AI skill overclaiming is particularly dangerous because the target audience (AI companies) can detect it instantly. Use the evidence chain: "What model did you use? How did you evaluate its output? What did you do when it was wrong? What is the difference between fine-tuning and prompt engineering?" The candidate's comfort with these questions reveals the actual skill level.
