# The Career Spark — Cultural Norms by Market

This reference file exists because resume conventions vary significantly across countries and cultures. A frontier model with strong cultural knowledge can infer many of these norms. A less capable model cannot and will default to US conventions, which may actively harm the candidate in non-US markets. This file provides explicit norms to prevent that failure mode.

**How to use this file:** When the candidate targets a non-US market, read the relevant section before drafting. Apply the norms without needing to infer them. If the target market is not listed here, search the web for current resume conventions in that country before proceeding — conventions change, and this file may not cover every case.

**Important:** These norms describe the general expectation. Individual companies, especially international ones, may follow different conventions. When in doubt, check the specific company's career page for clues about expected format.

## 1. United States

**Document name:** Resume (not CV, except for academic positions)

**Length:** 1 page for early career (under 10 years), 2 pages maximum for experienced candidates. Exceeding 2 pages is a negative signal.

**Photo:** Never. Discrimination liability makes photos a red flag for employers.

**Personal details:** Name, email, phone, LinkedIn. No date of birth, no marital status, no nationality, no gender. Including these raises legal concerns for the employer.

**Education:** Minimal for experienced candidates. University name, degree, year. No high school. No GPA unless exceptional and recent (under 5 years). For candidates without a degree, omit the section entirely rather than listing incomplete education — the absence is less damaging than the incomplete entry.

**Tone:** Direct, confident, achievement-oriented. Quantify everything possible. "Increased revenue by 30%" not "helped improve revenue." Active verbs, first-person implied (no "I").

**Format:** Clean, professional, conservative formatting. No colors, no graphics, no creative layouts unless the candidate is in design. ATS compatibility is critical — US employers heavily use automated screening.

**Special notes:** Age discrimination is illegal but common in practice. Avoid graduation dates older than 15–20 years unless the degree is from a prestigious institution. Do not list every role — compress early career to prevent date-based age calculation. "References available upon request" is outdated — do not include.

## 2. France

**Document name:** CV (not resume). The French CV is a different document with different conventions.

**Length:** 1–2 pages is standard. Complete career history is expected. Unlike the US resume, compressing or omitting roles may raise questions.

**Photo:** Common and expected, though declining in some industries. Professional headshot, business attire. Omitting the photo is acceptable in international/tech contexts but may feel incomplete in traditional industries.

**Personal details:** Name, address, email, phone. Date of birth is traditionally included but increasingly optional. Marital status and nationality were historically included but are now declining. Age discrimination is illegal in France (as in the US) but the cultural expectation of including DOB persists in some sectors.

**Education:** Prominent, especially for Grandes Écoles graduates. The prestige of the institution carries significant weight in France. List the institution name, the program, the year, and the diploma obtained. Classe Préparatoire should be listed if completed — it signals academic rigor. For non-French candidates, include explicit equivalences.

**Tone:** More measured than US conventions. Team accomplishments alongside individual ones. Avoid the aggressive self-promotion tone that works in the US — it reads as arrogant in French professional culture. "Contributed to" and "participated in" are acceptable where US conventions would demand "led" and "drove."

**Format:** Clean and professional. A subtle color accent is acceptable. Sections typically include: État civil (personal details), Formation (education), Expérience professionnelle (experience), Compétences (skills), Langues (languages), Centres d'intérêt (interests).

**Special notes:** The "Centres d'intérêt" (interests/hobbies) section is expected in France. It is not a throwaway — French hiring managers use it to assess cultural fit and personality. Sport, culture, volunteer work, and creative pursuits are all appropriate. "Cinema and reading" is generic. "Member of a local rugby club for 8 years, currently coaching the youth team" is specific and reveals character. For candidates writing in French, linguistic quality matters more than in English-language resumes — grammatical errors or awkward phrasing are judged more harshly.

## 3. United Kingdom

**Document name:** CV (not resume).

**Length:** 2 pages is the strong standard. 1 page is too short for experienced candidates. 3 pages is too long.

**Photo:** Not expected and increasingly discouraged.

**Personal details:** Name, email, phone, location (city, not full address). No date of birth, no marital status, no nationality. The UK follows anti-discrimination norms similar to the US.

**Education:** Include but do not lead with it unless the candidate is a recent graduate or from Oxbridge/Russell Group (where institutional prestige carries weight similar to French Grandes Écoles).

**Tone:** Professional but slightly less aggressive than US conventions. Achievement-oriented but with more context. The British understatement norm means that extreme superlatives ("world-class," "revolutionary") read as hollow or arrogant. "Delivered a successful migration" lands better than "spearheaded a transformative digital revolution."

**Format:** Clean, professional, no photo. A brief personal statement or profile at the top (2–3 sentences) is standard and expected. Reverse chronological order.

**Special notes:** UK employers increasingly use ATS systems. Do not include "References available upon request." For candidates targeting both UK and US roles, the UK CV and US resume are similar enough that minor adjustments suffice — mainly length (UK allows 2 pages more comfortably) and tone (UK is slightly more measured).

## 4. Germany (DACH Region)

**Document name:** Lebenslauf (curriculum vitae).

**Length:** 1–2 pages for the Lebenslauf. However, German applications traditionally include additional documents (Bewerbungsmappe): cover letter (Anschreiben), CV, certificates (Zeugnisse), and references.

**Photo:** Expected in Germany, Austria, and German-speaking Switzerland. Professional headshot, business attire. Placed in the upper right corner or on a separate cover page. Omitting it is increasingly acceptable in tech and international companies but may feel incomplete in traditional German companies.

**Personal details:** Name, address, date of birth, nationality, and marital status are traditionally included. This is more information than US/UK norms. Younger companies may not expect all of these.

**Education:** Very important. German employers value formal qualifications, certifications, and the specific institution. Apprenticeship (Ausbildung) is listed and valued. Academic titles (Dr., Prof.) are used in professional contexts and should appear on the CV.

**Tone:** Factual and precise. German professional culture values accuracy and thoroughness over salesmanship. Avoid superlatives. "Implemented a new reporting system that reduced processing time by 20%" is better than "drove a revolutionary transformation in reporting efficiency."

**Format:** Highly structured, often in table format. Dates in the left column, details in the right. Chronological (not reverse chronological in traditional format, though reverse chronological is gaining ground). Gaps in employment must be explicitly accounted for — German employers notice and ask about gaps.

**Special notes:** Zeugnisse (reference letters from previous employers) are a critical part of German job applications. German law requires employers to provide them, and they follow a coded language system where specific phrases have specific meanings. A candidate's Zeugnisse may need interpretation. The absence of Zeugnisse is a red flag.

## 5. Netherlands / Nordics

**Document name:** CV.

**Length:** 1–2 pages.

**Photo:** Generally not expected in the Netherlands and Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland). The region follows strong anti-discrimination norms.

**Personal details:** Minimal. Name, email, phone, LinkedIn. Date of birth and nationality are not expected.

**Education:** Important but not dominant. The egalitarian culture means institutional prestige carries less weight than in France, Germany, or the UK.

**Tone:** Direct, honest, and team-oriented. Nordic professional culture values humility and collective achievement. "Our team delivered..." is stronger than "I drove..." The concept of Janteloven (Nordic cultural norm against standing out) means that aggressive self-promotion is counterproductive. Achievements should be stated factually without superlatives.

**Format:** Clean, modern. A brief personal profile is common. Reverse chronological.

**Special notes:** English is widely used in professional contexts, especially in the Netherlands and Nordics. An English CV is acceptable for most roles in international companies. Language skills should note native language plus English level explicitly.

## 6. Japan

**Document name:** Rirekisho (履歴書) for the standard format, Shokumu Keirekisho (職務経歴書) for the detailed career record.

**Length:** The Rirekisho has a standardized single-page format available at convenience stores and online. The Shokumu Keirekisho is 1–3 pages of detailed career history.

**Photo:** Required on the Rirekisho. Formal photo, passport-style, taken at a photo booth or studio. Business attire.

**Personal details:** Name, address, date of birth, gender, contact information. Commute time to the office is sometimes included.

**Education:** Listed chronologically from high school through university. The institution name and the year of graduation are critical.

**Tone:** Formal, humble, team-oriented. Individual achievement claims are rare. "Contributed to the team's success in..." is the expected tone. Direct self-promotion is culturally inappropriate.

**Format:** Highly standardized. The Rirekisho follows a specific template that should not be creatively redesigned. The Shokumu Keirekisho allows more freedom but should remain professional and structured.

**Special notes:** Handwritten Rirekisho was the traditional expectation and still carries weight in conservative industries, though printed versions are now widely accepted. For international candidates applying to Japanese companies, a standard English CV may be acceptable at foreign-facing companies, but a Rirekisho shows cultural awareness and effort.

## 7. China

**Document name:** 简历 (Jiǎnlì).

**Length:** 1 page preferred, especially for online applications through platforms like Zhaopin, 51job, or Boss Zhipin.

**Photo:** Expected. Professional headshot.

**Personal details:** Name, phone, email, date of birth, gender, and ethnicity are traditionally included. Political affiliation (Communist Party membership) can be relevant for government and state-owned enterprise roles.

**Education:** Very important. University ranking (985, 211 designation) carries significant weight. Graduate education and certifications are valued.

**Tone:** Achievement-oriented with quantified results. Chinese resume culture has moved closer to US conventions in recent years, especially in the tech sector and private companies.

**Format:** Clean, structured. Platforms like Zhaopin have standardized templates.

**Special notes:** For candidates applying to Chinese companies from outside China, a Chinese-language CV may be expected. For candidates applying to international companies in China, an English CV is standard. The candidate's Hukou (household registration) status may be relevant for certain roles and cities.

## 8. India

**Document name:** Resume or CV (both terms are used).

**Length:** 2–3 pages is acceptable, often longer than US norms. Detailed descriptions of each role are expected.

**Photo:** Common, especially in traditional industries. Less expected in IT/tech.

**Personal details:** Name, phone, email, LinkedIn. Date of birth, marital status, and passport number are traditionally included but declining. "Declaration" sections (self-attesting that information is accurate) are common but increasingly seen as outdated by modern companies.

**Education:** Very important. Specific institution, percentage/CGPA, year. Board examination results may be included for early-career candidates. Professional certifications (CA, ICWA, etc.) are prominent.

**Tone:** Detailed and comprehensive. Indian resumes tend to be more descriptive than US resumes. Quantified achievements are valued but the contextual narrative around them is more expansive.

**Format:** Often includes a "Career Objective" or "Summary" section at the top, followed by technical skills, experience, education, and personal details.

**Special notes:** For IT professionals, a separate "Technical Skills" section with specific technologies, tools, and platforms is expected and may be the first thing a recruiter scans. Visa status and willingness to relocate are relevant for international applications.

## 9. Canada

**Document name:** Resume (English Canada) or CV (French Canada / Québec).

**Length:** 2 pages standard for experienced candidates.

**Photo:** Not expected. Similar anti-discrimination norms as the US.

**Personal details:** Name, email, phone, LinkedIn. No photo, no DOB, no marital status.

**Tone and format:** Very similar to US conventions, with slightly less aggressive self-promotion. Bilingual capability (English/French) is a significant asset for federal government and Québec-based roles and should be prominently listed.

**Special notes:** For Québec-based roles, a French CV following closer to French conventions may be expected. Federal government applications use a separate standardized format.

## 10. Australia / New Zealand

**Document name:** Resume or CV (both acceptable).

**Length:** 2–3 pages is standard. Australian resumes tend to be slightly longer than US resumes.

**Photo:** Not expected.

**Personal details:** Name, phone, email, LinkedIn. No DOB, no marital status. Visa status and work rights should be mentioned if the candidate is not a citizen/permanent resident.

**Tone:** Achievement-oriented but with an Australian cultural aversion to "tall poppy syndrome" (standing out too much). Factual confidence rather than aggressive self-promotion. "Delivered a 25% improvement in processing time" is better than "crushed it by transforming the entire processing paradigm."

**Format:** Professional profile section at the top, followed by key skills, experience, and education.

**Special notes:** "Referees" (references) with contact details are traditionally included at the bottom of Australian resumes, unlike the US convention of omitting them.

## 11. Brazil / Latin America

**Document name:** Currículo or CV.

**Length:** 1–2 pages.

**Photo:** Common in Brazil and many Latin American countries, though declining in international companies.

**Personal details:** Traditionally comprehensive — name, address, DOB, marital status, nationality, CPF number (Brazil). International companies may expect less personal information.

**Education:** Important, especially for competitive markets. MBA and postgraduate degrees carry significant weight.

**Tone:** Professional and achievement-oriented. The Brazilian professional culture values warmth and relationship-building, which can be reflected in a slightly more personal tone than US conventions.

**Special notes:** For Brazil specifically, the legal framework around hiring means some personal information (like CPF) may be required at the application stage even if it seems unusual by US/European standards.

## 12. Middle East (Gulf States)

**Document name:** CV.

**Length:** 2–3 pages is standard.

**Photo:** Expected in most Gulf countries.

**Personal details:** Comprehensive — name, address, DOB, nationality, visa status, marital status. Nationality is particularly relevant in Gulf countries where visa and employment regulations vary by country of origin.

**Education:** Important. International certifications and prestigious institutional names carry significant weight.

**Tone:** Professional, detailed, respectful of hierarchy. Achievement-oriented but less aggressive than US conventions.

**Special notes:** Visa status and current residency are critical information. Salary expectations may be included or expected. For expatriates, the candidate's current location, visa status, and ability to obtain a work permit are often the first things a recruiter checks.

## 13. General Principles Across Markets

**When in doubt, research the specific market.** These norms change. What was standard five years ago may be outdated. Search for current conventions before applying market-specific norms.

**International companies often follow hybrid conventions.** A US tech company's Berlin office may expect US-style resumes. A Japanese company's London office may expect UK conventions with some Japanese elements. Check the job listing's language and the company's career page for signals.

**Language matters.** A CV in the local language shows respect and cultural integration. A CV in English shows international orientation. The choice depends on the specific role and company. When both languages are relevant, a bilingual CV (or two versions) may be appropriate.

**The photo question is cultural, not universal.** Do not assume US norms (no photo) apply everywhere. Do not assume that including a photo is always appropriate either. Follow the specific market's convention.

**Tone calibration is the hardest part.** What reads as confident in the US reads as arrogant in Japan. What reads as appropriately humble in the Nordics reads as underselling in the US. The target market determines the tone, not the candidate's preference.

## 14. Multilingual Candidates Applying Cross-Border

When a candidate is applying in a language that is not their native one — a French professional targeting US companies, a Japanese engineer applying in London, an Indian developer targeting German firms — specific challenges arise that are distinct from the cultural norms of the target market.

### The language proficiency question

The resume itself is a language sample. If the candidate writes their resume in English and the English is awkward or contains errors, the hiring manager draws a conclusion before reading the content. The AI should ensure the language is fluent, natural, and free of non-native patterns (word order calques from the native language, false friends, preposition errors common to speakers of that language). This is not just proofreading — it is ensuring the resume does not signal "non-native speaker" in ways that trigger unconscious bias.

If the candidate has formal language certification (TOEFL, IELTS, Cambridge, DELF/DALF, JLPT), include it. If they have informal but strong evidence (self-taught through immersion, published work in the target language, lived in the target country), describe it concretely: "Professional working proficiency, self-taught through 15 years of English-language professional practice" is stronger than "fluent."

### Bilingualism as an asset

In most international roles, bilingualism is a significant advantage. Position it as such, not as a footnote. "Native French, professional English" is a language line. "Bilingual professional capable of leading teams and client relationships in both French and English" is a selling point.

For candidates who speak three or more languages, this is genuinely rare and should be prominent. Multilingual capability demonstrates cognitive flexibility, cultural adaptability, and the ability to operate across markets — all of which have direct professional value.

### The "will they fit our team" concern

Hiring managers may worry (consciously or not) about communication friction: will conference calls be difficult? Will written communication be clear? Will cultural differences create misunderstandings? The resume and cover letter should preempt this by demonstrating fluency through the writing itself. If the document reads naturally and confidently, the concern evaporates before it forms.

For remote roles specifically, written communication quality is the primary signal. A candidate whose resume and cover letter are crisp, clear, and natural in the target language has already demonstrated the most important capability for remote work.

### Localization is not translation

A French CV translated into English is not a US resume. The structure, tone, content emphasis, and cultural expectations are different. If a candidate needs both a French CV and a US resume, these should be built separately with reference to each market's norms, not translated from one to the other. The through-line and evidence are the same. Everything else adapts.
