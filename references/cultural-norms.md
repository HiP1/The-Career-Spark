# The Career Spark — Cultural Norms by Market

Explicit resume conventions by country. A frontier model can infer many of these. A less capable model will default to US conventions, which may harm the candidate. When the target market is not listed, search the web for current conventions before proceeding.

---

## United States

**Format:** Resume (not CV). 1 page early career, 2 max experienced. **Photo:** Never. **Personal details:** Name, email, phone, LinkedIn only. No DOB, marital status, nationality. **Education:** Minimal for experienced; no GPA unless exceptional and recent. **Tone:** Direct, confident, achievement-led. Quantify everything. Active verbs. **Notes:** Age discrimination common in practice. Avoid graduation dates older than 15–20 years. "References available upon request" is outdated.

## France

**Format:** CV. 1–2 pages, complete history expected. **Photo:** Common, declining in some industries. **Personal details:** Name, address, email, phone. DOB traditionally included, declining. **Education:** Prominent, especially Grandes Écoles. Classe Préparatoire signals rigor. **Tone:** Measured, team-oriented. "Contributed to" acceptable where US demands "led." **Notes:** "Centres d'intérêt" (interests) section expected and actually read. Specific > generic ("Member of rugby club 8 years, coaching youth" > "cinema and reading"). Linguistic quality judged harshly. Grammatical errors are worse than in English resumes.

## United Kingdom

**Format:** CV. 2 pages standard. **Photo:** Not expected. **Personal details:** Name, email, phone, city. No DOB/marital/nationality. **Education:** Include but do not lead unless Oxbridge/Russell Group. **Tone:** Professional, achievement-oriented but moderate. British understatement: "delivered a successful migration" > "spearheaded a transformative revolution." **Notes:** Brief personal statement (2–3 sentences) at top is standard.

## Germany (DACH)

**Format:** Lebenslauf. 1–2 pages plus Bewerbungsmappe (cover letter, CV, certificates, references). **Photo:** Expected. Professional headshot, upper right corner. **Personal details:** Name, address, DOB, nationality, marital status traditionally included. **Education:** Very important. Academic titles (Dr., Prof.) used professionally. Apprenticeship (Ausbildung) valued. **Tone:** Factual, precise. Avoid superlatives. **Notes:** Employment gaps must be explicitly accounted for. Zeugnisse (reference letters) are critical. They use a coded language system with specific meanings. Absence is a red flag.

## Netherlands / Nordics

**Format:** CV. 1–2 pages. **Photo:** Not expected. **Personal details:** Minimal. **Education:** Important but institutional prestige carries less weight (egalitarian culture). **Tone:** Direct, honest, team-oriented. Janteloven (Nordic norm against standing out): "Our team delivered..." > "I drove..." **Notes:** English widely used professionally. English CV acceptable for most international companies.

## Japan

**Format:** Rirekisho (standardized single page) + Shokumu Keirekisho (1–3 page career detail). **Photo:** Required. Formal, passport-style. **Personal details:** Name, address, DOB, gender. **Education:** Chronological from high school. Institution name critical. **Tone:** Formal, humble, team-oriented. Direct self-promotion culturally inappropriate. **Notes:** Standardized Rirekisho template should not be creatively redesigned. Handwritten still valued in conservative industries.

## China

**Format:** 简历 (Jiǎnlì). 1 page preferred. **Photo:** Expected. **Personal details:** Name, phone, email, DOB, gender. Political affiliation relevant for government/SOE roles. **Education:** Very important. University ranking (985, 211) carries weight. **Tone:** Achievement-oriented with quantified results, moving closer to US conventions in tech/private sector. **Notes:** Platforms like Zhaopin, 51job have standardized templates.

## India

**Format:** Resume or CV. 2–3 pages acceptable. **Photo:** Common in traditional industries, less in IT. **Personal details:** Traditional: DOB, marital status, passport number (declining). **Education:** Very important. Specific institution, percentage/CGPA. Professional certifications prominent. **Tone:** Detailed, comprehensive, more descriptive than US. **Notes:** For IT, separate Technical Skills section is expected and scanned first.

## Canada

**Format:** Resume (English) / CV (French Canada). 2 pages. **Photo:** Not expected. **Personal details:** Same as US. **Tone:** Similar to US, slightly less aggressive. Bilingual capability (English/French) prominent for federal/Québec roles. **Notes:** Québec may expect French CV closer to French conventions.

## Australia / New Zealand

**Format:** Resume or CV. 2–3 pages. **Photo:** Not expected. **Tone:** Achievement-oriented but with "tall poppy" aversion. Factual confidence, not aggressive self-promotion. **Notes:** "Referees" with contact details traditionally included at bottom (unlike US). Visa/work rights should be mentioned for non-citizens.

## Brazil / Latin America

**Format:** Currículo/CV. 1–2 pages. **Photo:** Common, declining in international companies. **Personal details:** Traditionally comprehensive (DOB, marital, CPF number in Brazil). **Education:** MBA and postgraduate carry significant weight. **Notes:** CPF may be required at application stage even if unusual by other standards.

## Middle East (Gulf States)

**Format:** CV. 2–3 pages. **Photo:** Expected. **Personal details:** Comprehensive: DOB, nationality, visa status, marital status. Nationality particularly relevant (visa/employment regulations vary by origin). **Education:** International certifications and prestigious institutions carry weight. **Notes:** Visa status and residency are often the first things a recruiter checks.

---

## General Principles

- International companies often follow hybrid conventions. Check job listing language and career page.
- Language choice: local language = cultural integration signal. English = international orientation.
- Photo norms are cultural, not universal. Follow the specific market.
- Tone calibration is the hardest part. What reads as confident in the US reads as arrogant in Japan.

---

## Multilingual Candidates Applying Cross-Border

The resume itself is a language sample. Ensure the language is fluent, natural, and free of non-native patterns. Include formal language certifications if available. Concrete evidence of proficiency ("15 years of English-language professional practice") > vague claims ("fluent").

**Bilingualism is an asset, not a footnote.** "Bilingual professional leading teams in both French and English" is a selling point, not a language line.

**Localization ≠ translation.** A French CV translated into English is not a US resume. Build separately for each market using each market's norms.
