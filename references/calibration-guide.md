## 1. The Calibration Target

Both underselling and overselling are calibration errors. They are not a spectrum where "somewhere in the middle" is good enough. Each has a specific failure mode:

- **Underselling:** The documents do not reflect what the candidate actually did. Hiring managers never see the real person.
- **Overselling:** The documents claim more than the candidate can support. The interview exposes the gap. Credibility damage is worse than never getting the interview.

The AI's job is to find the accurate framing: the version that is both fully honest AND maximally compelling. In practice:
- Corrections to underselling candidates push upward
- Corrections to overclaiming candidates push downward
- The target is the same: accuracy at the right altitude

**The fine line test:** Can the candidate explain and defend this claim in their own words, unprompted, in a live conversation? If yes, the framing is accurate. If no, it is overclaiming regardless of whether it is technically true.

## 2. Handling "Negative" Self-Descriptions

When a candidate offers a seemingly unflattering self-assessment, do not dismiss it. Do not immediately reframe it as a positive. Investigate the mechanism.

**Process:**
1. Accept the raw description without judgment
2. Ask: "How does that play out in practice? Has it ever conflicted with something else about how you work?"
3. Identify the underlying mechanism
4. Name the mechanism accurately — the CV-safe version is not the opposite of what the candidate said

**Examples from practice:**

| Raw Description | Mechanism Underneath | CV-Safe Framing |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| "I'm lazy" | Instinctive triage that prevents over-exploration | Efficiency-oriented decision making |
| "I'm an introvert" | Selective engagement from a position of social competence; excels in written/async contexts | Thrives in remote-first, written-communication environments |
| "I can't code at speed" | Natural review-and-direct mode that matches AI-assisted development | Review-and-direct working mode aligned with AI-augmented workflows |
| "I run out of problems" | Operations competence with a restless optimization drive | Seeks environments with unsolved structural challenges |
| "I'm too honest" | Intellectual honesty under pressure, even when it is politically costly | Trusted as an honest broker in multi-stakeholder situations |

**Why this matters:** The candidate's honesty about the raw trait is what makes the reframe credible. "I'm lazy but I did 470 manual test runs because the work demanded it" tells a hiring manager exactly when their quality standard overrides their efficiency filter. Document the tension, not just the traits.

## 3. Limitations the Market Is Catching Up To

Some friction points are not permanent limitations. They are early positions on a trajectory the industry is moving toward.

**The test:** Is this limitation genuinely constraining, or is the candidate's target industry in the process of dissolving it?

**Examples:**
- "I don't write production code at speed" → In an era where AI generates code and humans review/direct, this is a market-timing observation, not a gap
- "I don't have a CS degree" → In an industry that increasingly values demonstrated skill over credentials, this is becoming less relevant
- "I've never held a formal PM title" → If the candidate has done PM work under a different title for years, the gap is nomenclature, not capability

**Framing:** "Already where the industry is heading" is different from both "weakness" and "spin." Name the trajectory explicitly.

---

## 4. Friction Points That ARE the Methodology

Sometimes a candidate's limitations and their working method are the same thing viewed from different angles.

**The pattern:** If someone's natural mode is "review and direct rather than produce from scratch" and they have built a structured AI orchestration workflow around exactly that mode, the friction point and the methodology unify.

This is worth naming explicitly because it tells a hiring manager the working style is stable and self-consistent, not a compromise. The candidate is not trying to become something different. Their limitation is their methodology is their strength.

## 5. Competing Internal Instincts

The most revealing trait descriptions come from two forces pulling in opposite directions within the same person.

**How to probe:** "You said you're lazy — has that ever conflicted with something else about how you work?"

**What to document:** How the tension resolves in practice. Which instinct wins, under what conditions.

**Examples:**
| Instinct A | Instinct B | Resolution |
|------------|------------|------------|
| Laziness / efficiency filter | Quality standard / obsessive rigor | Quality wins when the work demands it; laziness prevents wasted effort on work that does not |
| Over-branching / exploration drive | "Good enough" stop signal | Laziness counterbalances over-branching; efficiency filter triages which branches to pursue |
| Introversion / social avoidance | Force multiplier / mentoring instinct | Engages deeply in structured contexts; avoids unstructured social interaction |

**Why this matters for hiring managers:** A candidate who can articulate their own operating manual — including the internal conflicts and how they resolve — is more valuable than one who cannot. Self-awareness at the senior level is itself a trait worth featuring.

## 6. Failure Patterns as Assets

Career-spanning failure patterns are at least as important as success patterns.

**Why:**
- A hiring manager will notice them even if the narrative does not name them
- Unaddressed failure patterns become quiet doubts that undermine the strongest claims
- Named and owned failure patterns become evidence of self-awareness and maturity
- They contain the clearest signal for role targeting — what to avoid next time

**How to surface them:** Look for recurring gaps between input and outcome.
- Built at the frontier repeatedly but never captured the value?
- Led teams organically but never held the title?
- Produced innovations that someone else commercialized?
- Created value for employers but not for themselves?

Ask directly: "What didn't work, and why?" The candidate's answer — especially the level of blame versus structural analysis versus acceptance — reveals how to frame it.

**How to use each failure pattern:** Turn it into three things:
1. An honest acknowledgment in the narrative
2. A prepared interview response with layered depth (first answer + deeper follow-up)
3. A role-targeting filter (what kind of organization avoids repeating the pattern)

---

## 7. Interview Response Layering

For every hard question (friction points, failure patterns, obstacles), prepare at least two layers:

**Layer 1 — First answer:** Honest, complete, shows self-awareness. This gets the nod.

**Layer 2 — Deeper follow-up if pushed:** Shows wisdom, structural thinking, maturity. This gets the respect.

**Example structure:**
- Q: "Why didn't you capture the value of your innovations?"
- Layer 1: "The vehicle was a small company with limited resources. My role was infrastructure builder, not business developer. The value I created went to clients and partners, not to equity I held."
- Layer 2 (if pushed): "Value capture requires a lot of things to align — right people, timing, place, price, and opportunity. I was strong on the building side but did not have the business development partner or the capital structure to capture what I built. That is specifically what I am looking for in my next role — an organization with the resources to deploy what I build."

**The test:** Does Layer 1 satisfy a fair interviewer? Does Layer 2 change their assessment of the candidate's maturity?

## 8. Market-Specific Calibration

If the candidate targets multiple markets, adjust for cultural expectations:

| Dimension | US Market | French/European Market |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Self-promotion tone | Direct, confident, achievements-led | More measured, team-oriented, avoid appearing arrogant |
| Education emphasis | Skills-first; degree matters less | Degree and institution prominent; Grandes Écoles carry weight |
| Resume length | 1–2 pages maximum | Full CV with complete history acceptable |
| Photo | Never (discrimination liability) | Common on French CVs, optional |
| Personal details | Minimal (no DOB, marital status) | Often included |
| Career gaps | Explain briefly | Explain more carefully |

**The tone calibration is the hardest part.** What reads as confident in the US may read as arrogant in France. What reads as appropriately modest in France may read as underselling in the US. Name the target market before calibrating.

## 9. Energy and Motivation Mapping

Do not rely solely on behavioral analysis to infer motivation. Ask the candidate directly:

- What kind of work gives you energy?
- What drains you?
- What do you actively enjoy versus what you can do but do not love?
- When were you most engaged at work? What was happening?
- What would make you quit a job?

These questions surface information that no external analysis can reach:
- The difference between "can code" and "can code but doesn't love it"
- The difference between "does operations well" and "does operations well but gets restless after the problems are solved"
- The difference between "leads teams" and "leads teams but thrives most as a force multiplier"

The answers become friction points, role-targeting filters, and the motivational core of the narrative. A resume that positions the candidate for work they find draining is a resume that sets up a bad hire — even if they get the job.
