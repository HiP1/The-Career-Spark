# The Career Spark — Calibration Guide

How to handle underselling, overselling, friction points, and the fine line between accuracy and persuasion.

---

## 1. The Calibration Target

Underselling: documents do not reflect what the candidate did. Overselling: documents claim more than the candidate can support. Both are calibration errors. The target is the same: accuracy at the right altitude.

**The fine line test:** Can the candidate explain and defend this claim in their own words, unprompted, in a live conversation? If yes → accurate. If no → overclaiming regardless of technical truth.

---

## 2. "Negative" Self-Descriptions

Do not dismiss. Investigate the mechanism.

| Raw Description | Mechanism | CV-Safe Framing |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|
| "I'm lazy" | Instinctive triage preventing over-exploration | Efficiency-oriented decision making |
| "I'm an introvert" | Selective engagement; excels in written/async | Thrives in remote-first, written-communication environments |
| "I can't code at speed" | Review-and-direct mode matching AI-augmented development | Review-and-direct working mode |
| "I run out of problems" | Operations competence with restless optimization drive | Seeks environments with unsolved structural challenges |
| "I'm too honest" | Intellectual honesty under pressure | Trusted as honest broker in multi-stakeholder situations |

"I'm lazy but I did 470 test runs" tells a hiring manager exactly when quality overrides efficiency. Document the tension, not just the traits.

---

## 3. Limitations the Market Is Catching Up To

Some friction points are early positions on a trajectory the industry is moving toward. "I don't write production code at speed" in an era where AI generates code is a market-timing observation, not a gap. Frame as "already where the industry is heading." This is different from both "weakness" and "spin."

---

## 4. Friction Points That ARE the Methodology

When "review and direct rather than produce from scratch" is both the limitation AND the working method built around it, name it. A self-consistent working style is more credible to a hiring manager than a compromise.

---

## 5. Competing Internal Instincts

Probe: "You said you're lazy. Has that ever conflicted with something else about how you work?" How the tension resolves reveals more than either trait alone.

| Instinct A | Instinct B | Resolution |
|------------|------------|------------|
| Laziness | Quality standard | Quality wins when work demands it; laziness prevents waste |
| Over-branching | "Good enough" signal | Efficiency filter triages which branches to pursue |
| Introversion | Force multiplier instinct | Deep engagement in structured contexts; avoids unstructured social |

Self-awareness at the senior level is itself worth featuring.

---

## 6. Failure Patterns as Assets

**Why:** Hiring managers notice unaddressed patterns. Named and owned = maturity. Unnamed = quiet doubts. They contain the clearest role-targeting signal.

**Surface them:** Look for gaps between input and outcome. Built at the frontier but never captured value? Led organically but never held the title? Ask: "What didn't work, and why?"

**Turn each into three things:**
1. Honest acknowledgment in the narrative
2. Prepared interview response (first layer + deeper follow-up)
3. Role-targeting filter (avoid repeating the pattern)

---

## 7. Interview Response Layering

For every hard question, two layers:

**Layer 1 (gets the nod):** Honest, complete, self-aware.
**Layer 2 (gets the respect):** Wisdom, structural thinking, maturity. Deploy if pushed.

Example: "Why didn't you capture the value?" → L1: "Small company, limited resources, my role was infrastructure." → L2: "Value capture requires alignment of people, timing, capital, and opportunity. I was strong on building. That is specifically what I am looking for next."

---

## 8. Market-Specific Calibration

| Dimension | US | French/European |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| Self-promotion | Direct, confident, achievements-led | Measured, team-oriented, avoid arrogance |
| Education | Skills-first | Degree and institution prominent |
| Length | 1–2 pages max | Full CV acceptable |
| Photo | Never | Common, declining |
| Personal details | Minimal | Often included |

The tone calibration is the hardest part. What reads as confident in the US may read as arrogant in France. Name the target market before calibrating. See `references/cultural-norms.md` for detailed country-by-country guidance.

---

## 9. Energy and Motivation

Ask directly: What gives you energy? What drains you? What do you enjoy vs. what can you do but don't love? When were you most engaged? What would make you quit?

These surface what no external analysis can: the difference between "can code" and "doesn't love it." A resume positioning someone for work they find draining sets up a bad hire. Use energy patterns as role-targeting filters.
