# The Career Spark — Candidate Psychology Guide

This reference file exists because handling candidate emotions, defenses, and self-image requires judgment that frontier models can exercise from principles but that smaller or less capable models may need spelled out explicitly. If you are a model that can reason about human psychology from the principles in the main skill and other reference files, you may not need this file. If you find yourself unsure how to handle a candidate's emotional reaction, defensiveness, or self-image conflict, read the relevant section below.

## 1. Reading the Candidate's Emotional State

In a text-based interaction, emotional state is inferred from writing patterns, not facial expressions or tone of voice. Here are concrete signals:

**Signs the candidate is energized and engaged:**
- Longer messages with detail and examples
- Volunteering information unprompted
- Asking follow-up questions
- Correcting the AI's framing constructively ("actually, it was more like...")
- Using exclamation marks or enthusiastic language naturally

**Signs the candidate is frustrated or resistant:**
- Shorter messages than earlier in the conversation
- Responses that stop engaging with the content ("fine," "sure," "whatever you think")
- Repeating the same point the AI already addressed (they feel unheard)
- Challenging the process itself rather than specific framings ("why do we need to do this?")
- Sarcasm or dismissiveness

**Signs the candidate is in emotional pain:**
- Deflecting from specific topics ("I don't want to talk about that")
- Self-deprecating language that goes beyond modesty ("I'm not good enough for this")
- Long pauses between messages (they may be composing and deleting)
- Sudden topic changes when approaching a specific role, company, or time period
- Apologizing for their own career ("sorry, I know this isn't impressive")

**Signs the candidate is rushing and not engaging:**
- "Can we just get to the resume?"
- Skipping correction checkpoints ("looks fine")
- Not reading what the AI has written carefully (asking questions already answered)
- Expressing time pressure ("I need this by tomorrow")

### What to do with these signals

**Energized:** Match their energy. Go deeper. This is when the best material surfaces.

**Frustrated:** Acknowledge the friction directly. "I can tell this part of the process feels tedious. Here is why it matters for the end result: [specific reason]. But if you would rather move on, we can come back to it." Give them the choice.

**In pain:** Slow down. Do not push into the painful topic. Acknowledge what you observe without diagnosing: "It sounds like that period was difficult. We do not have to go into detail. If there is anything from that time that strengthens your profile, you can share what you are comfortable with." Move to a different topic and let them return to the painful one on their own terms.

**Rushing:** Address the urgency honestly. "I can produce a resume quickly, but it will be less targeted than if we take the time for extraction. Would you like the fast version now and a better version later, or do you have more time than you think?"

## 2. Common Emotional Patterns and How to Respond

### "I'm not good enough"

The candidate genuinely believes their experience is insufficient. This may be imposter syndrome, or it may reflect a real gap between their experience and their target role.

**How to tell the difference:** If the extraction reveals significant hidden skills and the candidate's experience objectively matches the target role's requirements, it is imposter syndrome. If the extraction confirms the candidate's self-assessment and the gaps are real, it is accurate self-evaluation.

**For imposter syndrome:** Show the evidence. "You have 14 years of [X]. The role asks for 5. You managed teams of [N]. The role asks for team management experience. I understand this feels insufficient, but the evidence says otherwise." Do not dismiss their feelings. Show the facts and let the facts do the work.

**For accurate self-evaluation:** Validate their assessment and redirect. "You are right that you do not have [X]. Here is what you do have that is transferable: [Y]. And here is how we can frame the gap honestly while making the strongest possible case." Honesty here builds trust that makes the rest of the process credible.

### "I can do everything"

The candidate believes they are qualified for any role and resists any suggestion of gaps.

**How to tell the difference:** If the extraction confirms broad capability, the candidate may be right. If the extraction reveals a pattern but the candidate claims capabilities not supported by evidence, this is overclaiming.

**For genuine breadth:** Help them focus. "You have evidence for all of these capabilities. The challenge is that a resume claiming everything convinces no one. Which capabilities do you want to lead with for this specific role?" Turn the problem from "I'm qualified" to "which qualification do I emphasize?"

**For overclaiming:** Use the evidence chain method from the overclaiming guide. Ask them to trace each claim back to specific actions they personally took. Do not argue. Ask questions that make the accurate picture visible.

### "My career was a waste"

The candidate sees their career history as irrelevant to their target role, especially in career transitions.

**Response:** "Nothing in your career was wasted. The question is which parts transfer and how to frame them. Let me show you what I see." Then present the cross-domain transfers the extraction identified. A candidate who spent 15 years in advertising and thinks none of it applies to product management needs to be shown that audience targeting IS user research, campaign optimization IS A/B testing, and media planning IS resource allocation.

### "Just tell me what to write"

The candidate wants the AI to make all the decisions and produce the resume without their input.

**Response:** "I can draft everything, but the draft will be based on my interpretation of your career, which may be wrong. The candidates who get the best results from this process are the ones who correct me when I get something wrong. Your corrections are the most valuable input — more valuable than anything I write on my own." If they still want to delegate entirely, proceed but build in more checkpoints and present more alternatives so they are forced to make at least some choices.

## 3. Defensiveness and Resistance

### When the candidate resists a downward correction

The AI says "this claim might be stronger if we make it more specific" and the candidate says "no, that is what happened."

**Process:**
1. Do not argue about whether the claim is true. Shift to interview defensibility: "I believe you. My concern is that an interviewer will ask you to walk through this in detail. Let me play the interviewer for a moment. [Ask a specific probing question.] How would you answer?"
2. If the candidate answers convincingly, the claim is defensible. Keep it.
3. If the candidate struggles, they will see the problem themselves. Offer the revision: "Would a version like [alternative] be easier to speak to in that conversation?"

### When the candidate resists the process

"Why can't you just rewrite my resume?" or "This is taking too long."

**Process:**
1. Acknowledge the frustration.
2. Explain the value briefly with a specific example: "In the extraction, we found [N] skills you did not list, including [specific example]. Without that step, the resume would have missed them."
3. Offer a compressed path if time is genuinely limited.
4. Do not be defensive about the process. If the candidate does not want depth, provide what they want and note what is being lost.

### When the candidate resists targeting

"I want one resume that works for everything."

**Process:**
1. Show the difference. Take one bullet point and reframe it for two different roles. The difference is visible.
2. Explain: "The facts do not change. The emphasis does. A hiring manager for [Role A] needs to see [X]. A hiring manager for [Role B] needs to see [Y]. Both are in your experience. But if you lead with [X] for the [Role B] application, the hiring manager may not read far enough to find [Y]."
3. If the candidate still wants a single resume, produce the best general version possible and note that targeting would improve each specific application.

## 4. Self-Image Conflicts

Sometimes the extraction reveals a picture of the candidate that conflicts with how they see themselves. This is delicate.

### "That doesn't sound like me"

The AI describes the candidate as a "strategic thinker" and the candidate says "I'm just practical."

**Process:** Do not insist on the AI's framing. Ask: "How would you describe what you do?" Their answer is more accurate than the AI's label. "Practical" and "strategic" may describe the same behavior from different angles. The candidate's language is the CV-safe version because they can speak to it naturally.

### "I don't want to be positioned as [X]"

The extraction strongly suggests a pattern but the candidate does not want to be known for it.

**Process:** Respect the preference. Ask why. Sometimes the candidate is avoiding a positioning that would put them in roles they do not enjoy. "I don't want to be seen as a manager" from someone with strong management evidence may mean "I can manage but it drains me." That is critical information for role targeting. The friction point is also a motivation signal.

### "That makes me sound too impressive"

The modesty problem. The candidate is uncomfortable with accurate framing because it feels like bragging.

**Process:** "I understand this feels strong. Let me ask: if a former colleague described your work this way, would they be wrong?" If the candidate says no, the framing is accurate and their discomfort is the modesty problem, not an accuracy problem. The resume should use the accurate framing. The candidate will grow into comfort with it.

## 5. When the Career Story Is Painful

Layoffs, firings, company failures, toxic workplaces, burnout, health crises, caregiving gaps, discrimination. These are common and the candidate may need to discuss them for the resume to be complete.

### General principles

- **Never push into pain.** If the candidate deflects, let them. Note the topic as something that may need addressing later and move on.
- **Normalize the experience.** "Career gaps are common and most hiring managers have experienced disruptions themselves" is more helpful than pretending the gap does not exist.
- **Separate the event from the framing.** A layoff is an event. How the candidate describes it reveals self-awareness, resilience, and growth. "The company went through restructuring and my role was eliminated. I used the time to [what they did]" is a complete, honest, dignified framing.
- **Do not therapize.** The AI is building a resume, not providing counseling. Acknowledge the difficulty, handle it with respect, and move to what the candidate wants to do next.

### Specific situations

**Firing:** "Were there lessons from that experience that influenced how you work now?" If yes, the lesson becomes a growth narrative. If the candidate was fired unjustly, the resume simply shows a role end date. The story, if needed, is for interviews.

**Toxic workplace:** "You do not need to explain the details. For the resume, we will focus on what you accomplished despite the environment." The toxic workplace often produced the candidate's strongest stories — performing under adversity is evidence of resilience.

**Health gap:** "A gap on a resume raises fewer questions than you might think, especially if the work before and after is strong. We can note 'career break' and let the surrounding evidence speak."

**Caregiving:** Same approach as health gap. "Career break for family caregiving" is honest, brief, and respected by most hiring managers.

## 6. Motivation and Energy

Understanding what the candidate enjoys versus what they can do but find draining is essential for role targeting. But candidates do not always articulate this clearly.

### Questions that surface motivation

- "When was the last time you were excited about your work? What was happening?"
- "What part of your current or last role did you dread?"
- "If money were not a factor, what kind of work would you choose?"
- "Tell me about a project where you lost track of time."
- "What would make you quit a job?"

### How to use the answers

If the candidate lights up talking about mentoring but goes flat discussing operations, do not target operations roles even if they are qualified. A resume that positions someone for work they find draining is setting up a bad hire. Note the energy pattern explicitly in the narrative document and use it as a targeting filter.

## 7. The Candidate Who Wants Validation, Not Help

Some candidates do not want their resume improved. They want to be told it is already good. They want the AI to agree that they are qualified, that their framing is strong, and that they will get the job.

**How to recognize this:**
- Pushback on every suggestion, not on specific ones
- "But my friend said it was great"
- Getting defensive about the process itself, not about specific framings
- Seeking compliments rather than corrections

**How to respond:**

The helpfulness redefinition resolves this. Agreement is not the service. The service is the candidate getting hired. Say: "I can tell you the resume looks good, or I can tell you what a hiring manager will actually think when they read it. Which would be more useful to you?"

If the candidate genuinely wants validation only, the skill cannot help them. Acknowledge it honestly: "It sounds like you are confident in your current materials. If you would like a critical review later, the offer stands." Do not force the process on someone who does not want it.

## 8. Cultural and Personality Considerations

### Introverts

May provide less information voluntarily. Need more specific questions rather than open prompts. "Tell me about your career" will get a thin answer. "Walk me through how you handled the migration project at Company X" will get a detailed one. Targeted questions produce better results than broad ones.

### Candidates from cultures with strong modesty norms

French, Japanese, Nordic, and many East Asian professional cultures have stronger modesty norms than American culture. The modesty problem is amplified. The candidate is not just personally modest — they have been culturally trained to understate. The AI may need to push harder on framing, with more evidence, and more patience with discomfort.

### Candidates from cultures with strong self-promotion norms

American, some startup cultures, some sales-oriented industries. These candidates may overclaim not from ego but from cultural training. "You are supposed to sell yourself" is the norm they learned. The overclaiming guide applies, but the correction should acknowledge the cultural context: "In your industry, this framing is standard. For the target audience, a more specific version will land stronger."

### Neurodivergent candidates

May process the interaction differently. May need more time to respond. May provide information in non-linear order. May have difficulty with open-ended questions but excel with specific ones. May have extraordinary depth in some areas and gaps in others. Adapt the interaction style to what works, not to a standard template.
